# Spatial Horror Reference Board (Non-Implementation)

## Purpose
This document is a creative anchor, not an implementation spec. Its job is to preserve the game’s identity when development gets lost in systems, contracts, prefabs, networking, and debug work.

This board should answer:
- what this game is emotionally trying to do,
- what artistic lineage it belongs to,
- what it should borrow,
- what it should avoid,
- and what unresolved creative questions still need your direct input.

It is intentionally non-technical. Use it to keep the project’s **why** visible when the repo becomes dominated by engineering detail.

## Core creative thesis
This game is not just “a haunted house with procedural weirdness.” It is a spatial-horror game about trying to build orientation, selfhood, and safety inside a reality whose rules keep shifting under pressure.

The house should not simply be scary. It should feel unstable in a way that evokes:
- misrecognition,
- social rule drift,
- invalidated perception,
- recursive self-monitoring,
- and the exhaustion of trying to remain coherent inside systems that refuse to stay legible.

That is where the queer and neurodivergent interpretive layer matters. The horror is not only “monster in the dark.” It is also “the map keeps changing while other people still expect you to navigate flawlessly.”

## Reference role
This board is for:
- art direction conversations,
- mood alignment,
- narrative/thematic calibration,
- feature triage,
- creature and anomaly taste checks,
- and deciding whether an idea belongs to *this* game.

It is **not** for writing system contracts or implementation plans.

## Primary inspirations

### P.T.
What seems useful:
- repeating domestic architecture that becomes less trustworthy over time,
- a small spatial footprint that gains terror through variation rather than scale,
- psychological pressure built through recurrence and anticipation,
- and the way mundane space becomes unbearable once the player stops trusting it [web:92][web:93][web:102].

What to borrow:
- the loop as a tension engine,
- environmental recurrence with meaningful deviation,
- restrained use of impossible architectural continuity,
- and dread created by waiting for wrongness rather than constant action [web:92][web:102].

What to avoid:
- overreliance on obscure trigger logic,
- puzzle gating that feels like random ritual rather than player reasoning,
- and copying its exact hallway grammar such that our game loses its own identity [web:92][web:93].

### House of Leaves
What seems useful:
- architecture as psyche,
- the house as a contradiction engine rather than just a location,
- labyrinth as identity pressure,
- and the idea that space changes through experience, interpretation, and fear [web:95][web:98][web:101].

What to borrow:
- impossible interiority,
- emotional charge in measurement and mismeasurement,
- the feeling that exploration becomes obsession,
- and the sense that the house resists stable narration [web:95][web:101].

What to avoid:
- trying to mechanically reproduce the novel’s textual tricks directly,
- over-explaining the metaphysics of the house,
- and making the experience so abstract that the player loses immediate playable stakes [web:95][web:98].

### The Exit 8
What seems useful:
- tiny space, huge tension,
- anomaly detection as concentrated player attention,
- route repetition that sharpens memory instead of dulling it,
- and a rule set simple enough that small deviations become intensely meaningful [web:94][web:97].

What to borrow:
- observational play,
- confidence erosion through subtle environmental change,
- the pleasure of noticing spatial wrongness,
- and minimalism as a force multiplier [web:94][web:97].

What to avoid:
- turning the whole game into a spot-the-difference exercise,
- flattening horror into pure anomaly checklist gameplay,
- and letting the player relationship to the house become too abstractly gamey [web:94][web:97].

## Secondary inspirations

### Alien: Isolation
What seems useful:
- pressure generated by one dangerous intelligence shaping player behavior,
- the emotional rhythm of preparation, retreat, risk, and overcommitment,
- and the feeling that environment navigation changes when a stalking threat is present.

What to borrow:
- the predator as pacing force rather than constant spectacle,
- a strong hide/advance rhythm,
- and the way a hostile presence can make familiar routes feel newly hostile.

What to avoid:
- overcommitting to stealth at the expense of spatial-horror identity,
- or letting the creature become the entire game while the architecture becomes background.

### Lovecraftian and cosmic horror traditions
What seems useful:
- wrongness beyond easy explanation,
- scale contradiction,
- unstable reality,
- and fear of systems that exceed the human frame.

What to borrow:
- the sense of encountering a structure too large or inconsistent for ordinary reason,
- and the emotional texture of awe + dread.

What to avoid:
- generic tentacle aesthetics,
- lore dumping,
- and flattening cosmic horror into cliché “ancient evil” wallpaper.

### Liminal-space / backrooms-adjacent aesthetics
What seems useful:
- familiarity drained of comfort,
- transit spaces as emotional traps,
- and the anxiety of repetition without progress [web:94][web:97].

What to borrow:
- unease in thresholds, corridors, and transition spaces,
- and a sense that utility architecture can become spiritually wrong [web:94][web:97].

What to avoid:
- becoming just another backrooms imitation,
- visual sameness across every space,
- and internet-creepypasta shorthand replacing real thematic intent.

## Core things this game may be “about”
These are hypotheses, not final truths.

Potential thematic lanes:
- identity under unstable rules,
- queer self-construction inside hostile or incoherent social architecture,
- neurodivergent pattern recognition as both gift and burden,
- safety as temporary local order,
- the cost of needing certainty in an environment built on contradiction,
- the difference between private reality and shared consensus,
- and the fear of being “wrong” in a space that keeps changing its answer.

These feel promising because they tie spatial mechanics to lived meaning rather than treating horror as surface decoration.

## Core things the game should probably avoid becoming
- A generic ghost-hunting co-op game with fancier hallways.
- A pure jumpscare game.
- A pure stealth game.
- A pure anomaly-spotting game.
- A puzzle game where players brute-force arbitrary triggers.
- A lore-heavy cosmic-horror wiki project.
- A trauma simulator that mistakes pain for depth.
- A game whose metaphor is so vague it could mean anything.

## Candidate identity pillars
These are draft statements that can later become a tighter inspiration charter.

### 1. Spatial betrayal with rules
The world shifts, but not randomly. There are hidden principles behind its movement.

### 2. Recognition as survival
The player survives by noticing, remembering, and comparing.

### 3. Reality pressure over combat fantasy
The game should feel like enduring and interpreting a hostile environment, not mastering enemies through power escalation.

### 4. Emotional specificity over generic horror
The game should feel authored by your perspective, not by broad horror moodboards.

### 5. A house that reflects contradiction
The architecture should feel personal, social, and psychic all at once.

## Aesthetic tensions to resolve
These are useful because your strongest identity may emerge from how you answer them.

- Domestic house vs institutional asylum space.
- Cozy-wrong vs cold-hostile.
- Queer domestic traces vs total depersonalization.
- Human-scale dread vs cosmic-scale vastness.
- One iconic stalker vs mostly environmental horror.
- Slow suffocation vs sudden rupture.
- Subtle anomalies vs dramatic impossible transformations.
- Decay/mold/organic creep vs sterile overclean surrealism.
- Intimate autobiographical tone vs abstract symbolic tone.

## Open creative questions for you
Answering these will help sharpen the project’s why.

### Personal connection
1. What is the deepest emotional truth you want this game to express?
2. When you say “madness angle,” what do you actually mean—and what do you want to avoid implying?
3. What parts of queer experience feel important here: secrecy, performance, code-switching, chosen family, rule instability, surveillance, alienation, desire, shame, transformation?
4. What parts of neurodivergent experience feel central: sensory overload, pattern fixation, social ambiguity, burnout, masking, recursive self-monitoring, dissociation, need for stable rules?
5. Do you want the game to feel autobiographical, allegorical, or only lightly inspired by lived experience?

### House identity
6. What kind of place is the house at baseline: suburban home, inherited family house, institutional residence, halfway house, care facility, old queer commune, impossible hybrid?
7. Should the house feel like it once loved its inhabitants, or like it was always indifferent?
8. Is it more important that the house feels personal, oppressive, holy, sick, parasitic, empty, or hungry?
9. What are the 3 most iconic rooms the game must have?
10. What kind of hallway is “your hallway” for this game—narrow and domestic, clinical and fluorescent, wooden and old, carpeted and sound-dead, wet and concrete?

### Horror taste
11. What kind of fear do you most want: dread, confusion, humiliation, panic, awe, grief, loneliness, obsession, violation, helplessness?
12. What kind of horror do you personally find cheap or boring?
13. How much body horror belongs here, if any?
14. Should the entity be seen clearly, glimpsed, half-understood, or mostly inferred?
15. Should the game have one primary creature, many possible entities, or none until late?

### Player fantasy
16. What is the player fantasy besides “escape”? Investigator, witness, survivor, caretaker, trespasser, pilgrim, exile, mourner?
17. What do you want co-op to *mean* emotionally: mutual validation, fractured trust, asymmetrical perception, shared burden, loneliness despite company?
18. Should co-op make the house more stable, or should it create new forms of instability?
19. If one player sees something another does not, should that feel like betrayal, mystery, or ordinary collaboration?
20. What kind of stories do you want players to tell afterward?

### Spatial identity
21. What kinds of geometry changes excite you most: loops, stretch corridors, impossible interiors, room substitution, one-way overlap, off-by-one doors, false return, duplicated rooms?
22. What kinds of geometry changes feel too gamey or too hard to read?
23. How subtle should the first 15 minutes be?
24. When should the game first become openly impossible?
25. How often should the player be certain they are right?

### Tone and aesthetics
26. What textures or materials feel emotionally correct: damp wallpaper, old wood, fluorescent tile, mold, velvet, plastic, peeling paint, carpet, concrete?
27. What sounds belong here: HVAC hum, footsteps above, distant voice, ringing pipes, queer domestic music traces, whispered mimicry, total silence?
28. Is there any humor here, or none?
29. What color temperatures belong to “safe,” “uncertain,” and “wrong”?
30. What real-world places, buildings, apartments, institutions, or homes should the art direction pull from?

### Boundaries
31. What themes are off-limits or need careful handling?
32. Are there representations of madness or asylum imagery you explicitly do **not** want to reinforce?
33. How metaphorical should the relationship between identity and space be?
34. How much explicit story should exist versus environmental implication?
35. What would make this project feel exploitative or dishonest to you?

## Questions for narrowing the creature or threat
1. Is the threat a hunter, a witness, a mirror, a parasite, a curator, or a caretaker gone wrong?
2. Does it want the players dead, lost, changed, sorted, split apart, or absorbed?
3. Should it feel ancient, intimate, domestic, institutional, divine, or alien?
4. Does it obey house rules, create the rules, or exploit them?
5. Is it one being across many manifestations, or many manifestations of one underlying condition?

## Questions for narrowing the artifact/seal objective
1. Why do artifacts exist in the house at all?
2. Are they anchors, wounds, lies, protections, memories, or infestations?
3. Does destroying them heal the house, expose it, anger it, or merely let you leave?
4. Should players feel righteous destroying them, or conflicted?
5. What does “return the house to normal” actually mean—and is that even true?

## Questions for narrowing the jam-scale identity
1. What is the one sentence pitch that could only belong to this game?
2. What is the one mechanic that must be fun in the first prototype?
3. What is the one image a player should remember a week later?
4. What is the one emotional aftertaste you want at credits or session end?
5. If you had to cut 70 percent of current ideas, what survives?

## Temporary direction hypotheses
These are not final, but they may help you react.

### Hypothesis A — Queer domestic labyrinth
A house full of lived-in traces, chosen-family residue, tenderness, and contradiction slowly becomes impossible, as if intimacy itself has been reclassified as unstable.

### Hypothesis B — Institutional unreality
A care-like or asylum-adjacent environment keeps changing its logic while claiming to be stable, making the horror about systems that pathologize the person trying hardest to make sense of them.

### Hypothesis C — Personal house, cosmic interior
A recognizable home contains a reality far larger than it should, using human-scale emotional detail at the threshold of something unreasonably vast.

## Current best-fit ingredients
Based on the pivot so far, the most promising mix may be:
- domestic-recognizable rooms,
- observational anomaly play,
- looped or stretched circulation,
- one stalking pressure source at most,
- artifacts as destabilizing anchors,
- and a thematic frame centered on unstable social/spatial rules.

That mix feels distinct enough to stand apart from a clone while still being buildable at MVP scale.

## How to use this doc
Return to this document when:
- a feature sounds cool but may not fit,
- creature ideas start drifting generic,
- level art loses thematic specificity,
- the house starts feeling like “any horror house,”
- or the technical roadmap begins to replace the creative thesis.

If a system cannot be connected back to this board’s why, it may be technically impressive but creatively off-course.
